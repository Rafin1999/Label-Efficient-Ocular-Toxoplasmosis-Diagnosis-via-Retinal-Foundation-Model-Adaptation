# Label-Efficient-Ocular-Toxoplasmosis-Diagnosis-via-Retinal-Foundation-Model-Adaptation

Ocular toxoplasmosis (OT) is the leading cause of posterior uveitis worldwide, yet automated diagnosis from fundus images is constrained by small, imbalanced datasets. We present a systematic study of label-efficient OT classification across four controlled experiments spanning eight architectures, five label fractions (5--100\%), seven augmentation strategies, and five class-imbalance strategies, evaluated on two independent datasets. A central finding is that fine-tuning protocol, not architecture alone, determines whether retinal foundation models are usable at this data scale. Standard single-phase fine-tuning collapsed RETFound-DINOv2 to majority-class prediction with zero active-lesion recall, whereas a two-phase strategy combining frozen-backbone warm-up with layer-wise learning-rate decay (LLRD) restored balanced three-class prediction and raised the macro area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) from 0.666 to 0.982. This recipe was paradigm-specific: the same protocol collapsed the MAE-pretrained RETFound, which instead required single-phase LLRD. Under the appropriate protocol, RETFound-DINOv2 achieved the highest AUC of any model at the 25\% label fraction (0.939), while ConvNeXt-Base was strongest at full labels (AUC = 0.993). On an external hospital cohort, binary screening AUCs exceeded internal three-class AUCs for every model, with three architectures reaching 1.000; we attribute this to task simplification and the small, balanced external test set rather than unconditional generalisation. Augmentation sensitivity was architecture-dependent: CLAHE improved ConvNeXt-Base (+0.041 AUC at 25\% labels) but degraded RETFound-DINOv2, and mixing-based augmentation harmed performance under label scarcity. For class imbalance, oversampling was most effective from 25\% labels onward, whereas focal loss was counterproductive when minority and majority diseased classes shared visual features. These findings provide budget-aware, empirically grounded configurations for OT screening and identify fine-tuning protocol as a decisive, paradigm-specific design choice in foundation-model adaptation for rare, imbalanced medical imaging.
